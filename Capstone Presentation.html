The Gamma Regression Model

The Gamma Distribution

A continuous random variable \(\textit{Y}\) follows a gamma distribution if its probability density function is given by

\[\begin{gather*} f(y|\alpha, \lambda) = \frac{\lambda^{\alpha} y^{\alpha - 1} e^{- \lambda y}}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \textbf{1}_{(0,\infty)}(y) \end{gather*}\]

where \(\alpha\), \(\lambda\) \(>\) 0, \(\Gamma\)(.) denotes the gamma function, and \(\textbf{1}\)(.) is the indicator function.

The Gamma Distribution

For this parameterization:

Mean: \(E(\textit{Y}) = \alpha/\lambda\)

Variance: \(Var(\textit{Y}) = \alpha/\lambda^2 = \mu^2/\alpha\)

Support: \(\textit{x} \in (0, \infty)\)

\(\textit{Y} \sim \textit{G}(\mu, \alpha)\) denotes \(\textit{Y}\) follows a gamma distribution with \(E(\textit{Y}) = \mu\) and \(\alpha\) shape parameter.

The gamma distribution is a generalization of the Chi-square distribution with n degrees of freedom.

The Gamma Distribution

Re-parameterization: As function of mean (\(\mu\)) and shape parameter (\(\alpha\)) is given by

\[\begin{gather*} f(y|\mu, \alpha) = \frac{1}{y \Gamma(\alpha)} \Bigl(\frac{\alpha y}{\mu}\Bigr)^{\alpha} e^{-\alpha y/\mu}\textbf{1}_{(0,\infty)}(y) \end{gather*}\]

where \(\mu, \lambda >\) 0, \(\Gamma(.)\) denotes gamma function, and \(\textbf{1}(.)\) is the indicator function.

\(\textit{Y} \sim \textit{G}(\mu, \alpha)\)
Mean: \(E(\textit{Y}) = \mu\)
Shape parameter: \(\alpha\) (Roussas 2014)

The Gamma Regression Model

Gamma regression is a statistical model that predicts a numerical value.
Dependent variable: numerical
Predicting Variables: binary, discrete, or continuous

Let \(\textit{Y}_i \sim \textit{G}(\mu_i, \alpha)\), \(i = 1,\cdots,\textit{n}\), be a random sample of size \(\textit{n}\).
Let \(\textit{g}\) be the link function \(\textit{g}(\mu_i) = \mathit{x^{\prime}_i} \beta\)
with \(\beta = (\beta_0,\cdots,\beta_d)^{\prime}\) the vector of mean regression parameters
\(\textit{x}_i\) is the i-th vector value. (Bossio and Cuervo 2015)

The Gamma Regression Model

We have the following representation:


\(ln(\hat{Y}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \cdots + \beta_d X_d\), where
\(X_1, \cdots, X_d\) are predictors and

\(\beta_0, \cdots \beta_d\) the parameters.


So if \(X_i\) changes by 1, the prediction of \(\textit{Y}\) multiplies by \(e^{\beta_i}\).

Gamma regression is typically used for skewed data.

Gamma Regression in R

We use the General Linear Model (glm) function.

For simple Gamma regression with a logarithmic (log) link to fit data, the following code should be used:

model_1 <- glm(Y ~ X1 + X2 + X3 + X4, data = data_1,
           family = Gamma(link = "log"))

The ‘link’ option is an indicator that modifies our algorithm.

The R glm model uses the Fisher Scoring algorithm to fit its regression model to the data.

Gamma Regression in the prediciton of Rent and Income

  • Linear Regression is a popular choice to model all kind of numerical values.
  • “for most data-sets, income can … be calculated by the traditional linear regression” (Kibekbaev and Duman 2016).
  • Dependent variable is a ratio
  • The distribution is skewed
  • In this case we use gamma regression to predict data

Conclusion

Concluding Observations

Major factors considered in this study that affected housing-income ratio:

  • Dwelling type

  • Household composition

  • Education of the household head

  • Area density

  • Time in current dwelling

  • The year the survey was taken

Concluding Observations


Some other factors considered:

  • Race

  • Public Use Microdata Area

  • And several interactions of these variables

These factors were not significant and negatively affected our model.

Concluding Observations

Rent burden was elevated by certain predictors:

  • Apartment buildings

  • Living alone

  • A family without a marriage unit at the helm

  • Recently moved

  • 2021 rent burdens are significantly higher than 2016

However higher education lowered rent burden significantly.

Future Research

Suggestions for Future Research

  • This study was limited in part by the method used to gather data.

  • A more specific survey would help us clear any misunderstandings in our data.

  • Different model approached could be taken with this same set of data.

  • A Logistic Regression model targeting households with ‘Rent Burden’ as in this 1995 study (Oh 1995) would reduce the complexity of the model.

Suggestions for Future Research


  • Allow for expansion of the model’s predictors.

  • A Beta Regression model would also be ideal for modeling the proportion of housing costs versus income.

  • A more long term study with data from multiple decades could expand our knowledge of housing burdens.
